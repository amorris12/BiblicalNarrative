      Purpose

      Jesus’ faithful journey to the cross and his triumphant resurrection are the center of God’s redemptive plan for our salvation. The purpose of this chronological narrative is to tie all the details provided in each of the four gospels seamlessly together into one cohesive narrative and create a picture of each Jesus’ final days.

      We can have complete confidence in God's Word. As we see the perfect harmony between the four Gospels, it eliminates doubt and builds our faith. We can also appreciate the full context of each event in Jesus' final days, so we don't miss out on anything God is saying to us.

      The beginning of each section lists the scriptures references used. A “|” is used to denote where the text moves into the next reference.       AD 30?

      To summarize the excellent work of Dr Floyd Nolen Jones, upon careful examination of the scriptures and reflection on first century historical sources we can be confident in identifying the crucifixion and resurrection of Jesus in the year AD 30. Luke 3:23 and Luke 3:1 tell us that Jesus was about thirty years old in the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar when he was baptized by John. The fifteenth year of Tiberius’ reign of Judea from Roman sources equates to AD 26. Jesus' age at this date matches perfectly with dating His birth in 4 BC. This dating is based on astromonical calcuations of an eclipse recorded by first century Jewish historian Josephus that occurred just before the death of Herod the Great.

      The book of John records Jesus participating in 4 passovers, the first which coming in the spring of AD 27 after Jesus’ baptism in AD 26. Therefore, we can place Jesus’ crucifixion in AD 30.

      This dating is affirmed perfectly by Daniel's prophecy (chapter 9) that there would be 483 years from the decree to restore and rebuild Jerusalem to the death of the annointed one. The decree given to Nehemiah to rebuild the wall can be dated to 454 BC, based on persian records. 454 BC + 30 AD = 484 less 1 for going from BC to AD = 483 years.

      Good Thursday?

      In Matthew 12:40 Jesus said, “the Son of Man will be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth.” Traditionally we have honoured Jesus’ crucifixion for our sins on Good Friday, because the Gospels record that the next day was a Sabbath. However, if Jesus died on a Friday it would not have been three days and three nights to Resurrection Sunday. We can get three days by counting inclusively (which was typically for the jews), but it is only two nights.

      There is an easy solution to this problem. John 19:21 tells that not only was the next day a Sabbath, but it was a “special” Sabbath. This is a reference to Leviticus 23:6,7. The Feast of Unleavened Bread begins the day after the Passover lamb is sacrificed on the 15th of the first month and this day is a Special Sabbath where no one works. Therefore, since Jesus was crucified the day before the feast began and the feast always begins with a Sabbath (no matter the day of the week), Jesus did not have to die on a Friday. In fact we see that Jesus died on a Thursday, so they actually had two Sabbaths in a row, which explains why the women waited until Sunday to bring the spices to the tomb.
